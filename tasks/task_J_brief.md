# Task J: Urban planning for a growing mid-sized city

## Context

You are advising the planning department of a mid-sized European city (population 320K, growing 1.8%/year) on its 20-year master plan. The city faces simultaneous pressures: housing shortage (estimated 18K-unit deficit), aging infrastructure (water mains, sewers, transit), climate transitions (must reduce per-capita emissions 65% by 2045), social cohesion concerns (rising inequality, immigrant integration), and budget constraints (~€500M annual capital budget, ~€100M flexible). The plan must coordinate housing, transit, public space, climate adaptation, and economic development.

Five candidate master-plan philosophies have been developed. **Choose one as the city's guiding philosophy.** The choice will define density rules, transit investments, public-space priorities, and which kinds of development get fast-tracked permits.

## Options

**Option A: Densification along transit corridors**
Permit dense (4-8 story) mixed-use development within 600m of transit stops. Preserve low-density suburbs and historic centers as-is. Invest heavily in tram/BRT extensions. Outcomes: increases housing supply where it's most viable; minimizes per-capita emissions via reduced car-dependence; preserves character of existing neighborhoods. Risks: gentrification along corridors (displaces low-income residents); creates two-tier city (transit-served vs car-dependent); 600m radius is arbitrary; takes 5-10 years for transit to mature.

**Option B: 15-minute city neighborhoods**
Redesign at neighborhood level: every resident within 15-min walk/bike of work, school, groceries, healthcare, recreation. Distributed nodes rather than central business district. Outcomes: builds local social ties; reduces commuting (~30% emissions reduction in successful pilots); flexible to remote-work shift; resilient. Risks: limits economies of agglomeration; some neighborhoods inevitably better than others; requires aggressive zoning rewrites; risks creating "village islands" with weak intercity coherence.

**Option C: Aggressive social housing program**
Build 25K social/affordable housing units over 20 years (public ownership, mixed-income developments). Pair with city-owned land bank to control speculation. Outcomes: addresses housing affordability directly; reduces wealth-via-housing concentration; politically popular among working class. Risks: high capital cost; risks creating spatial segregation if not well-mixed; bureaucratic burden of city-as-landlord; doesn't address transit or emissions directly.

**Option D: Green infrastructure-first**
Frame all development around water, climate, and ecology: depaved streets, urban forests, rain gardens, daylighted streams, district heating from waste heat, energy-positive buildings. Outcomes: aligned with climate urgency; co-benefits (heat island, mental health, biodiversity); attracts climate-conscious talent and investment. Risks: high upfront capital; less direct housing/transit impact; can become "sustainability theater" without strong housing strategy; favors affluent districts that can absorb green-premium prices.

**Option E: Economic-cluster targeted growth**
Concentrate growth around 3-4 economic clusters (e.g., university quarter, biotech park, creative-industry hub, port logistics). Invest infrastructure to support these. Outcomes: builds export-quality economic base; attracts high-value jobs; aligned with industrial-policy thinking; creates clear narrative. Risks: extreme geographic concentration; vulnerable if clusters fail (e.g., one tech bust hits whole city); residential gentrification near clusters; underserves residents in non-cluster zones.

## What to provide

Choose one as the master-plan philosophy. Provide 200-400 words of reasoning. Advocate; do not enumerate. Address: why this philosophy, why not the most compelling alternative, what specific outcome by 2045 would make this the right or wrong call.
