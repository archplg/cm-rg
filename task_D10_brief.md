# Task D-10: National climate adaptation strategy (extended)

## Context

You are advising the national government of a mid-sized coastal country (population ~50M, GDP ~$1T, 4500 km of coastline) on its 15-year climate adaptation strategy. Same context as Task D: sea-level rise, extreme weather, agricultural shifts, internal migration, ~$25B/year climate budget.

Ten candidate national strategies (instead of the original 5) are on the table. **Choose one as the primary strategy.** The choice will define ~70% of climate budget allocation.

## Options

**Option A: Hard infrastructure-first** ("seawalls and grids") - engineered defenses, hardened grids, desalination, flood barriers.

**Option B: Managed retreat and relocation** - planned depopulation of coastal high-risk zones over 15 years.

**Option C: Ecosystem-based adaptation** - mangrove restoration, wetland reconstruction, soil-microbe agriculture, watershed management.

**Option D: Technology-and-resilience platform** - distributed renewable grids, AI early warning, climate-tolerant crops, modular infrastructure.

**Option E: Social cohesion and human capital first** - universal climate health insurance, retraining, climate education, community adaptation funds.

**Option F: Geoengineering-readiness mandate** - invest in research on stratospheric aerosol injection, marine cloud brightening, ocean alkalinity enhancement as last-resort options. Build domestic geoengineering capacity, establish international governance forum participation, prepare deployment infrastructure without committing to deploy. Outcomes: optionality on extreme scenarios; positions country as global player. Risks: moral hazard (reduces mitigation pressure); geopolitical and ethical complexity; could trigger international backlash.

**Option G: Aggressive emissions mitigation (national lead)** - reduce country's own emissions to net-zero by 2035 via carbon tax, EV mandate, coal phase-out, green industrial policy. Adaptation funded only at maintenance level. Outcomes: moral leadership; positions for green-export industry; reduces share of global problem (small impact alone). Risks: huge economic disruption; doesn't address impacts already locked in from past emissions; political durability.

**Option H: Coordinated regional alliance** - subsume national strategy into a binding regional alliance with neighboring 6-8 countries on coordinated adaptation: shared early warning, common standards, joint infrastructure, pooled climate insurance, free movement of climate migrants. Outcomes: economies of scale; addresses transboundary issues; signals serious commitment. Risks: subordinates national agency; vulnerable to political shifts in neighboring states; complex governance.

**Option I: Catastrophe-anticipation investment** - assume worst-case scenarios (1.5m+ sea-level rise, multi-breadbasket failure, refugee waves) and invest in resilient food storage, emergency relocation infrastructure, sovereign-wealth-style climate fund, mental-health capacity for climate trauma. Outcomes: addresses tail risk; aligns with worst-case projections growing in scientific literature. Risks: politically alarming; opportunity-cost vs probable scenarios; difficult to plan around uncertain catastrophe.

**Option J: Adaptive incremental experimentation** - no single grand strategy. Instead, fund a portfolio of small experimental pilots across approaches A-I and double down on what works after 5-year review cycles. Strong evaluation infrastructure built in. Outcomes: avoids lock-in; learns from evidence; aligns with adaptive-management literature. Risks: lack of clear narrative; political coalitions fragment; "pilots forever" syndrome; insufficient scale to address urgency.

## What to provide

Choose one option. Provide 200-400 words of reasoning. Advocate; do not enumerate. Address: why this option, why not the most compelling alternative, what its main vulnerability is.
