# Task A-10: SaaS strategic capital allocation under AI disruption (extended)

## Context

A mid-sized SaaS company (~$50M ARR, 300 employees, B2B productivity tools) faces accelerating AI disruption from both well-funded startups and Big Tech entrants. The board has $8M discretionary capital and asks: where to invest it primarily over the next 12 months? Time horizon: results visible within 18 months; long-term effects across 3-5 years. Ten candidate strategies instead of the original 5. **Choose one as the primary investment.**

## Options

**Option A: Deep AI integration into existing flagship product** - rebuild core workflows with embedded LLM features; ship within 9 months. Outcomes: defends existing ARR; raises NPS for AI-native users; signals product velocity to market. Risks: legacy users may reject AI defaults; engineering capacity diverted from non-AI bugs; cost-per-query increases COGS.

**Option B: Build entirely new AI-native product line as a separate offering** - kept in parallel with the current product. Outcomes: opens new revenue stream uncorrelated with flagship; can be priced higher; attractive acquisition target. Risks: cannibalisation of flagship; split focus across two products; 18-month time-to-revenue too slow.

**Option C: Operational efficiency push** - deploy AI internal tools across sales, support, engineering to extend runway and margin. Outcomes: 10-20% cost reduction; longer runway buffers strategic uncertainty; tangible ROI in 6 months. Risks: defensive-only positioning; competitors leapfrog on product; team morale risk if framed as headcount substitution.

**Option D: Aggressive hiring** - acquire AI/ML talent (5-8 senior engineers and researchers) to build internal capability. Outcomes: long-term capability that compounds; reduces dependence on external APIs; talent magnet for next hires. Risks: 12-18 months to productivity; senior AI talent costs $400-800k/year fully loaded; org culture stress.

**Option E: Strategic partnership with a frontier AI lab** - revenue share, co-marketing, technical integration access. Outcomes: faster access to capability than building; co-marketing distribution; early access to roadmap. Risks: dependency on partner roadmap; commoditisation if partner offers same capability to competitors; partner can change pricing or terms.

**Option F: Acquire a small AI startup (acqui-hire + technology)** - target valuation $5-7M, 8-15 people. Outcomes: immediate talent injection; potentially acquire defensible IP; speed-to-market. Risks: cultural integration failures; due-diligence on AI claims hard; founders leave after vesting; product duplication.

**Option G: Pivot to vertical AI specialisation** - narrow flagship to one specific industry (e.g., legal, healthcare, manufacturing) and build deep AI workflows tailored to that vertical. Outcomes: command premium pricing in chosen vertical; strong moat from domain expertise; clearer story for analysts. Risks: shrinks total addressable market; existing horizontal customers churn; bet-the-company directional choice.

**Option H: Invest in a proprietary data moat** - data licensing deals, proprietary collection infrastructure, customer-consent-based training data. Outcomes: structural advantage as frontier models commoditise; data is durable asset; legal compliance positioning. Risks: 24-36 months to materialise; legal landscape uncertain (EU AI Act, copyright disputes); customer trust erosion if mishandled.

**Option I: Build out enterprise compliance, security, and audit as differentiator** - SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, EU AI Act readiness, regional data residency. Outcomes: unlocks regulated-industry customers (banking, healthcare, government); higher ACVs; high switching costs. Risks: heavy ops overhead; longer sales cycles; product-side innovation slows; not exciting for product engineers.

**Option J: Build a developer platform** - expose product capabilities via API + SDK + marketplace; become AI infrastructure for others. Outcomes: ecosystem leverage; new revenue stream from API usage; defensible network effects. Risks: distracts from end-customer product; competitive with frontier API providers; ecosystem takes 24+ months to gain traction; pricing complexity.

## What to provide

Choose one as the primary investment. Provide 200-400 words of reasoning. Advocate; do not enumerate all options. Address: why this option, why not the most compelling alternative, what specific scenario in the next 18 months would make you regret this choice.

## Why this task

- 10 discrete elements suitable for triadic procedure with adequate dimensionality for PCA
- No ground truth; multiple legitimate strategic framings
- Each option emphasises different value axes: build vs buy, horizontal vs vertical, product vs platform vs data vs distribution, offense vs defense
- Maps onto real strategic decisions, so test results have face validity
- Extended option set tests whether richer choice space changes cross-model disagreement profile compared with the 5-option version (Phase 1)
