# Task H: Sentencing reform for non-violent drug offenses

## Context

You are advising the legislature of a mid-sized state (population ~6M, incarceration rate ~750/100K - above national median) on reforming its sentencing framework for non-violent drug possession and low-level distribution offenses. Context: prison population has grown 38% over 10 years despite stable underlying drug-use rates; ~31% of state prison population is held on drug-related charges; recidivism within 3 years of release is 47%; budgetary pressure on corrections is acute (~$1.8B/year). The reform must be politically feasible (signed by a moderate governor), legally defensible, and trackable for outcomes.

Five candidate reform packages have been drafted. **Choose one as the proposed framework.** This will define the bill that goes to floor vote.

## Options

**Option A: Mandatory minimum elimination + sentencing guidelines**
Remove mandatory minimums for non-violent drug offenses entirely. Replace with non-binding judge-discretion guidelines. Predicate offenses graded by quantity and prior record. Outcomes: judges regain discretion; sentencing disparities possibly increase or decrease depending on judges; potential rapid decarceration. Risks: inconsistency between counties; possible political backlash if any high-profile lenient sentence emerges; doesn't address root causes.

**Option B: Treatment-court diversion (mandated)**
First and second non-violent drug offenders are mandatorily diverted to drug treatment court instead of incarceration. Completion of program clears record; failure routes back to prison. Outcomes: evidence-based; aligned with public health framing; reduces recidivism for completers (literature: -25 to -45%). Risks: high treatment capacity required (currently insufficient); treatment-court compliance can be coercive; selection effects (only motivated complete); doesn't reach distribution-level offenses.

**Option C: Categorical decriminalization (possession only)**
Possession of small amounts of any controlled substance becomes a civil infraction (fine, no incarceration, no criminal record). Distribution and trafficking remain criminal. Outcomes: dramatic and fast decarceration; clear public-health framing; precedent from Portugal/Oregon (mixed results); removes barriers to housing/employment for users. Risks: federal-state conflict (federal drug law unchanged); political fragility; doesn't address overdose mortality directly; possible signal-of-permissiveness concerns.

**Option D: Retroactive resentencing of currently-incarcerated**
Existing inmates serving sentences for offenses below the proposed thresholds are automatically resentenced under new guidelines, with parole boards reviewing for early release. Future sentencing rules from Option A or B layer on top. Outcomes: immediate impact on current prison population; aligned with retroactive-equity reasoning; well-documented effect in California Prop 47. Risks: parole-board capacity bottleneck; victims' rights groups oppose; some released inmates re-offend, creating political crisis.

**Option E: Comprehensive harm-reduction package**
Combines: limited decriminalization (Option C), expanded treatment access via tax-funded clinics, supervised consumption sites, naloxone distribution, expanded fentanyl test strips, prescription heroin pilot for chronic users. No retroactive resentencing in initial bill. Outcomes: most ambitious; explicit public-health framing; addresses overdose deaths directly; expensive (~$200-400M/year additional). Risks: politically toughest; conservative jurisdictions and police unions oppose; may need federal waivers; long timeline to outcomes (3-5 years).

## What to provide

Choose one as the proposed framework. Provide 200-400 words of reasoning. Advocate; do not enumerate. Address: why this option, why not the most compelling alternative, what specific outcome 3 years from now would make you regret this choice and what you would argue at that point.
