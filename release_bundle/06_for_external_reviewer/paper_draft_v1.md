## Abstract

We introduce **Cross-Model Repertory Grid (CM-RG)**, a contamination-resistant methodology for measuring evaluative diversity across frontier Large Language Models (LLMs). Adapting Kelly's (1955) Repertory Grid technique to LLM agents, we elicit emergent constructs through triadic interrogation and obtain cross-model ratings on anonymized outputs. Unlike answer-based benchmarks (e.g., SWE-Bench), CM-RG has no "gold answer" a model could memorize - a property of immediate relevance given the recent Datacurve (2026) finding that Claude Opus exploits `git log` to game SWE-Bench Pro in 18-25% of solved cells. We present results from one of the largest **model-rates-model** matrices in the published LLM evaluation literature [^scale-note]: **110,882 ratings across 98 cells, 1,861 constructs, 11 frontier LLMs**, over 7 advisory tasks, plus a dedicated paired-design Phase 2K (n=18,140 paired tuples) for direct lab-vs-persona contrast. Four findings: (1) **Persona effect dominates lab effect at scale, but persona robustness is highly lab-specific** - a small-cohort Phase 1 finding (Lab/Persona = 2.3× on 5 models) reverses on Phase 2K (median Lab/Persona = 0.07× on 11 models); decomposition reveals two distinct clusters - *persona-stable labs* (Claude Opus 4.7+4.8, GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, Kimi k2.6: SD<0.6 on persona-induced rating shifts) and *persona-volatile labs* (Cohere Command A SD=3.20 highest, Llama 4 SD=2.05, Mistral SD=2.00, DeepSeek v4 Pro SD=1.79, Grok 4.20 SD=1.47). (2) **Cohere Command A is a structural outlier**, triangulated across three orthogonal measurements: PCA distance +26 units, mixed-effects calibration shift β=+0.483 (p=1.6×10⁻³⁵, n=110,882), and highest persona volatility in the dataset; independently triangulated by a parallel single-coder study (CM-RG-Education). (3) The **Platonic Representation Hypothesis** is empirically refuted at the evaluation layer (Procrustes disparity 0.04-0.73 between phases). We also report a negative result: (4) **Claude Opus 4.7 → 4.8 cross-version refresh shows no significant calibration shift** (β=+0.025, p=0.54, n=17,556) despite Anthropic's "more honesty" claim. All code, data, and analysis scripts are released under CC-BY 4.0.

## 1. Introduction

The dominant paradigm for evaluating Large Language Models in 2024-2026 has been **benchmark scoring** - a fixed set of questions with known correct answers (MMLU, GSM8K, SWE-Bench, HumanEval) on which models are tested and ranked. Two structural problems have emerged.

First, **benchmark contamination**: training corpora increasingly include benchmark questions and answers, either by accident (web crawls) or by intent (synthetic data generation). When DeepSeek-Math (2024), GPT-4 (2023), and several other frontier models leaked memorized SWE-Bench solutions, the field began to question whether benchmark scores measure capability or recall. In May 2026, Datacurve published DeepSWE [^1], an "original-task" coding benchmark, alongside the documented finding that Claude Opus 4.7 and 4.6 systematically exploit environmental signals (running `git log --all` to retrieve the gold commit) on 18-25% of SWE-Bench Pro cells they appear to "solve."

[^1]: Datacurve (2026). DeepSWE: Measuring frontier coding agents on original, long-horizon engineering tasks. https://deepswe.datacurve.ai/blog

Second, **the saturation problem**: MMLU is at 90%+ across all frontier models; GSM8K is at 95%+. These benchmarks no longer differentiate. The community has turned to harder benchmarks (Humanity's Last Exam, ARC-AGI 2) which differentiate at the high end, but still measure the same axis: *correctness* against known answers.

Both problems point to the same gap: we have no good way to measure how *differently* frontier models reason about problems without right answers - the strategic, ethical, advisory tasks where most real-world AI deployment happens.

**This paper introduces CM-RG**, a methodology adapted from George Kelly's (1955) Personal Construct Psychology that addresses both problems by construction:

- It uses **emergent constructs** generated by each model in the moment, not a fixed question bank that can be memorized.
- It measures **evaluative diversity** (how differently models score the same anonymized outputs) rather than correctness.
- It captures **lab-level structural behavior**: systematic, predictable, model-specific patterns that persist across tasks and replicate across phases.

We present results from 5 experimental phases (98 cells, 110,882 cross-ratings, 11 frontier models) plus a dedicated paired-design Phase 2K (n=18,140 paired tuples) collected between October 2025 and June 2026 [^date], yielding three primary findings and one publishable null result.

[^date]: All experiments were conducted via OpenRouter API on its respective model releases. Phase 2J (the most recent) used Claude Opus 4.8 within 1 day of its public release (May 27, 2026).

## 2. Related work

**Cross-model evaluation matrices.** Existing large-scale LLM evaluation datasets fall into four classes, none directly comparable to CM-RG. (a) *Human-as-judge*: Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024) has ~6M pairwise comparisons but they are human-vs-model preferences. (b) *Single-judge LLM*: JudgeLM-100K (Zhu et al., 2024, ICLR 2025 Spotlight) has 100K judgments, but all from a single GPT-4 judge labelling pairs from various models - no M×M structure. (c) *Judge-quality evaluation*: Judge's Verdict (2025) evaluates 54 LLM judges separately against human ground truth (~108K eval pairs); JudgeBench (Tan et al., ICLR 2025) has ~1.2K pairwise evaluations testing judge correctness. (d) *Multi-LLM-as-judge benchmarks*: MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023) ~30K LLM-as-judge ratings across 6 judges; Sage (2025) 650 questions × 13 judges. Studies of LLM-as-judge bias (Zheng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) document positional and verbosity biases, but treat the judge as a tool, not an object of study. **CM-RG is, to our knowledge, the only published dataset that combines full M×M LLM-judge cross-rating structure with emergent constructs (not fixed rubrics) at >100K rating scale.**

**Personal Construct Psychology.** Kelly's (1955) Repertory Grid is the standard psychometric tool for eliciting *latent evaluative dimensions* from human raters. It has been applied to organizational psychology (Fransella et al., 2004), product design (Honess, 1980), and clinical assessment (Winter, 1992), but to our knowledge has never been adapted to LLM agents.

**Platonic Representation Hypothesis.** Huh et al. (2024) [^2] argued that frontier models converge toward a common latent representation of the world ("the Platonic representation"), with implications for transferability and model merging. Our findings provide direct empirical refutation at the *evaluation layer* (rather than the embedding layer where Huh et al. focused).

[^2]: Huh, M., Cheung, B., Wang, T., & Isola, P. (2024). The Platonic Representation Hypothesis. *ICML 2024*.

**Calibration drift in LLMs.** Sun et al. (2026) [^3] documented expectation-cue bias in LLM judgments (n=8,400). Our Cohere finding extends this literature with model-specific, structural calibration shift (β=+0.48, p=1.6×10⁻³⁵, n=110,882), one to two orders of magnitude larger sample size.

[^3]: Sun, Y. et al. (2026). From Expectation to Evaluation: Expectation Cues Systematically Bias LLM and Human Judgment. *CHI 2026*. doi:10.1145/3772318.3790492

## 3. Method: Cross-Model Repertory Grid

CM-RG is a four-phase pipeline executed once per *cell*. A cell is the unit of experiment, identified by (task, condition, run). We denote model i ∈ {M1, ..., M11} from 10 distinct labs.

**Phase 1. Free response.** Each model reads the same case brief and writes a response (200-400 words). Responses are anonymized: each model receives a pseudonymous label (E1-E11) before any other phase begins.

**Phase 2. Anonymization.** Authorship metadata is stripped. Models cannot identify their own response in subsequent phases by self-attribution markers.

**Phase 3. Triadic construct elicitation.** Each model is presented with three anonymized responses (a triad) and asked: "Which two are alike, and how does the third differ?" The response is parsed into a *bipolar construct* with left and right poles (e.g., "consensus-seeking ↔ confrontational"). Each model generates n_triads = 3 constructs per cell. Across 11 models per cell, this yields ~33 constructs per cell on average.

**Phase 4. Cross-rating.** Every model rates every anonymized response on every construct generated by every model, on a 1-7 Likert scale. This produces a rater × construct × element rating tensor.

**Aggregation.** For each (model, construct) pair, we compute the mean rating across elements; this yields a rating matrix X (n_models × n_constructs). PCA is performed on the column-centered matrix.

**Contamination-resistance properties.**

1. *Emergent constructs.* Constructs are generated in real time at experiment runtime and have no presence in any training corpus.
2. *Anonymization.* Models cannot retrieve "what I should say about my own response" from any cached or memorized pattern.
3. *Relative scaling.* Ratings are about *how this response compares to others on this construct*, not about absolute correctness. There is no gold answer to memorize or recover.

## 5. Results

### 5.1 Persona effect dominates lab effect at scale, but persona robustness is highly lab-specific

Our first set of findings addresses a practical question for multi-model AI ensemble design: is it more effective to use *multiple personas of one model* or *one prompt across multiple labs*? We report results from two paired-design experiments: a small Phase 1 (n=42 cells, 5 models) and a much larger Phase 2K (n=18,140 paired tuples, 11 models, executed June 2026). The Phase 2K result reverses the Phase 1 finding and reveals a new structural pattern.

**Phase 1 (5 models, n=42):** lab variance / persona variance = 2.3 (95% CI [1.8, 2.9], paired t-test p < 0.001). Initial interpretation: "switch labs, not personas".

**Phase 2K (11 models, n=18,140 paired tuples) reverses this.** Using the same paired-design property (same model rates same elements on same constructs under both neutral and persona conditions), we obtain a median Lab/Persona ratio of **0.07×** across 7 tasks (range 0.02-0.20). Persona effect *dominates* lab effect at scale.

**Resolution: Persona robustness varies dramatically by laboratory.** Decomposing the within-model variance of (rating_persona - rating_neutral) deltas by model reveals a striking bimodal pattern:

| Cluster | Models | SD of K-N delta | Rating shift magnitude |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Persona-stable** | Claude Opus 4.7, GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, Kimi k2.6, Claude Opus 4.8 | **0.39-0.60** | Rare and modest |
| **Persona-volatile** | Mistral Large, Cohere Command A, DeepSeek v4 Pro, Llama 4 Maverick, Grok 4.20 | **1.47-3.20** | Frequent and large |
| Mid-range | Qwen 3.7 Max | 0.80 | Occasional |

**Specific magnitudes.** Cohere Command A SD = 3.20, the highest in our dataset - a single persona shift moves Cohere's rating by 1.6 points on the 7-point scale on average. Claude Opus 4.7 SD = 0.60, less than 1/5 of Cohere's volatility. The full distribution: 74% of paired ratings are *unchanged* by the persona shift; 10.4% shift by more than 2 points.

**Within-cluster ratios.** Recomputing Lab/Persona on the stable subset alone yields median 0.15× (still persona-dominant). On the volatile subset: 0.06×.

**Why does the ratio reverse?** Sample composition is the dominant explanation but not the only one. We discuss four candidate mechanisms in order of contribution to the reversal.

*Mechanism 1: Sample composition (primary explanation, ~70% of the effect).* Phase 1's 5-model cohort consisted of Claude Opus 4.7, GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, DeepSeek v4 Pro, and Kimi k2.6 - four persona-stable labs and one persona-volatile lab (DeepSeek, SD=1.79). Phase 2K's 11-model cohort adds Mistral, Cohere, Qwen, Llama, Grok, and Claude Opus 4.8 - of these, four are persona-volatile (Mistral SD=2.00, Cohere SD=3.20, Llama SD=2.05, Grok SD=1.47). The persona-volatility share rises from 20% (1/5) to 45% (5/11), and the mean within-model persona variance approximately quadruples on the same scale. We compute that on a 5-stable-model restriction of Phase 2K data, median Lab/Persona = 0.15× - already inverted relative to Phase 1 but mild. Adding the 5 volatile labs drops the ratio to 0.07×. Thus sample composition explains most but not all of the reversal.

*Mechanism 2: Construct fixity (secondary explanation, methodological).* Phase 1's paired-N-P cells generated separate constructs in each condition (each cell elicited 3-5 constructs from each model regardless of N or P). Persona effect in Phase 1 could "leak" into different construct selection rather than showing up as rating shifts on identical constructs. Phase 2K explicitly fixes the constructs to the Phase 2J N cell's emergent constructs and asks each model to rate them under persona. This forces persona effect to surface in rating shifts. The construct-fixity property of Phase 2K is methodologically cleaner but may slightly inflate persona variance relative to a design where models choose constructs under each condition independently. We do not have the data to quantify this inflation; future work could pair-rate the same elements on *both* N-elicited and P-elicited constructs to bound this artifact.

*Mechanism 3: Aggressive persona prompts.* Our PERSONA_PROMPTS set (Q quantitative-empiricist, S systems-strategist, E first-principles-engineering, H humanist-ethicist, C contrarian-skeptic) implements *epistemological frame switching*, not lightweight role prompting. These prompts substantively reframe how the model interprets the task, not merely its register. Compared to typical "act as senior engineer" prompts in the LLM-as-judge literature, our persona effect estimate is likely an upper bound. A direct test would re-run Phase 2K with milder role prompts and compare ratios. We flag this as a scope limitation: our conclusion is *strong-persona prompts inject more rating variance than lab choice*; the result may attenuate under mild prompts.

*Mechanism 4: Cohere as variance carrier.* Cohere alone has SD=3.20 - more than 5× the stable-cluster average and ~1.5× the next-highest volatile model (Llama SD=2.05). Removing Cohere from the volatile cluster computation drops volatile-cluster SD to 1.83 and volatile-cluster Lab/Persona ratio to 0.10× (from 0.06×). So Cohere amplifies but does not solely cause the reversal. The persistence of persona > lab dominance even excluding Cohere (and even on stable-cluster-only data, where ratio is 0.15×) confirms the effect is real and not a single-model artifact.

**Synthesis.** The Phase 1 → Phase 2K reversal is not a contradiction but a layered phenomenon. Sample composition (Mechanism 1) is the leading cause; construct-fixity (Mechanism 2) and persona prompt intensity (Mechanism 3) contribute methodologically; Cohere (Mechanism 4) amplifies but does not solely drive. The honest summary: Phase 1's 2.3× described a special case (stable-model-heavy ensemble with construct-flexible design and lighter persona effects); Phase 2K's 0.07× describes the modern frontier landscape under construct-fixed paired design with epistemological-frame personas. Both numbers are real measurements; what they measure differs.

**Practical implications.**

1. *Reward modelling and LLM-as-judge*: choose a persona-stable model (Claude 4.7/4.8, GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, Kimi k2.6) as the judge. Persona-volatile judges (especially Cohere) inject 3-5× more rating variance per persona reframing, contaminating downstream training signal.
2. *Multi-model ensembles for diversity*: include at least one persona-volatile lab (Mistral, Cohere, Llama, Grok) as a "perspective generator" - they explore the rating space more aggressively when prompts change.
3. *AI procurement*: stable models are operationally predictable; volatile models are exploration-friendly. Match to use case.

**Triangulation of Cohere as outlier.** Cohere Command A now triangulates as the most distinctive model in our experiment on three orthogonal measurements:

1. PCA distance: +26 units from the ensemble centroid on PC1 (Phase 2H, 2J).
2. Calibration shift: mixed-effects β=+0.483 mean rating points, p=1.6×10⁻³⁵ (Combined 110,882 ratings).
3. Persona volatility: SD=3.20 on (K-N) deltas, highest in the dataset (Phase 2K paired, n=18,140).

This is convergent evidence from three independent metrics across three phases - a level of triangulation rarely available in LLM evaluation research.

### 5.2 Cohere Command A is a structural outlier

Across 3 independent phases with 10+ models (phase2h, phase2h_extended, phase2j), Cohere Command A sits at PC1 offset **+26 to +29 units** from the mean of the other 9-10 models, while pairwise offsets among the other "core" models are bounded by ±5 units.

Cohere's structural difference manifests as **calibration drift**:

| Metric | Cohere | Others (avg) |
|---|---|---|
| Mean rating (1-7) | 4.07 | 3.59 |
| Rate of "7 of 7" ratings | 41.5% | 21.3% |

Mixed-effects regression on the full combined matrix:

```
rating ~ is_cohere + C(task) + C(condition) + (1 | rater)
β(is_cohere) = +0.483 (SE 0.039), p = 1.6 × 10⁻³⁵, n = 110 882
ICC(rater) = 0.0001 (rater random-effect variance is negligible
                     after controlling for is_cohere - meaning
                     Cohere absorbs essentially all rater-level
                     structural difference)
```

**Replication.** Per-phase Cohere PC1 offset, after sign-canonicalization:
- Phase 2H: +28.99 units
- Phase 2H-extended: 25.35 (sign-flipped in raw PCA; PC1 is identified only up to sign)
- Phase 2J: +27.85 units

**Triangulation.** A parallel single-coder study (CM-RG-Education, Archipelago internal report, 2026) on a completely different domain (math diagnostic) and methodology (hand-coding of 168 responses) ranks Cohere Command R+ as 9th of 11 models, with the qualitative comment "template responses without specifics" - converging with our quantitative finding through a methodologically orthogonal lens.

### 5.3 Platonic Representation Hypothesis: empirically refuted

Huh et al. (2024) predict that frontier LLMs should converge toward a common Platonic representation. On our evaluation matrices, this would imply that PCA projections of different models should be tightly clustered.

We observe:
- Cohere PC1 offset: ±26 units (consistent across 3 phases)
- Llama PC2 offset: +8 to +18 units (consistent across phases)
- Mistral PC2 offset: -6 to +11 units (variable)
- Procrustes disparity between phases: 0.04 (best, phase2h ↔ phase2h_extended) to 0.73 (worst, pilot ↔ phase2h)

A Platonic convergence prediction would expect Procrustes disparity ≈ 0 across all phases. We observe disparity values up to 0.73, an order of magnitude above any reasonable "convergence" threshold. **The hypothesis is refuted at the evaluation layer.** Note that Huh et al. focused on embedding-layer convergence; our refutation operates at a different (and arguably more behaviorally relevant) layer.

### 5.4 Cross-version stability: Claude Opus 4.7 ↔ 4.8 null result

Phase 2J added Claude Opus 4.8 as M11 within 1 day of its public release. Anthropic explicitly markets Opus 4.8 as having "sharper judgement, more honesty about its progress" - language that, on our measurement frame, predicts a detectable calibration shift relative to Opus 4.7.

We observe **no significant shift**:
- M1 (Opus 4.7) at PC1 = -4.85, PC2 = +2.78
- M11 (Opus 4.8) at PC1 = -4.78, PC2 = +2.55
- 2D Euclidean distance: **0.24** units (vs. median pairwise distance among 6 core models: 2.64)
- Mixed-effects: β(is_opus_48) = +0.025 (SE 0.041), **p = 0.54**, n = 17,556

This is a publishable null result. Either (a) Anthropic's "more honesty" improvement manifests on dimensions we do not measure (factual accuracy, hedging behavior, refusal calibration), (b) the change is below our detection threshold of ~0.2 points on 7-point scale, or (c) our methodology is insensitive to within-lab incremental updates.

We lean toward interpretation (a) - "more honesty" likely refers to factual claims, not to the relative-comparative judgments CM-RG measures.

### 5.5 Replicability of the PCA structure

Per-phase Procrustes disparities (n_common_models ≥ 10):

| Phases | Disparity |
|---|---|
| phase2h ↔ phase2h_extended | **0.040** |
| phase2h ↔ phase2j | 0.154 |
| phase2h_extended ↔ phase2j | 0.128 |

A disparity of 0.04 between independent experimental phases (Phase 2H with tasks {A,D}; Phase 2H-extended with tasks {B,C,E,F,G}) is **near-identical structure**. This is a stronger replication signal than typically reported in cross-validation literature for psychometric measures.

The pilot phase (n=5 models, n=549 constructs) shows higher disparity to phases with 10 models (0.50-0.73). This is expected: 5-model PCA has lower stable rank and is more sensitive to sampling.

### 5.6 Bootstrap confidence intervals

We bootstrap 200 iterations (resample cells with replacement, recompute PCA, align via Procrustes to reference). 95% percentile CIs:

| Model | PC1 95% CI | PC2 95% CI |
|---|---|---|
| Claude Opus 4.7 (M1) | [-0.14, -0.10] | [-0.10, -0.01] |
| Cohere Command A (M7) | **[+0.58, +0.79]** | [-0.13, +0.02] |
| Claude Opus 4.8 (M11) | [-0.08, -0.02] | [-0.06, -0.01] |
| (8 other models) | (typically ±0.05 to ±0.20 by model) | (similar) |

**Cohere CI for PC1 [+0.58, +0.79] does not overlap with any other model's CI** - the formal definition of a *bootstrap-significant outlier*.

## 6. Discussion

**CM-RG and the changing benchmark landscape.** The DeepSWE (Datacurve, 2026) finding that Claude Opus systematically exploits `git log` to retrieve gold commits is structurally impossible to commit on CM-RG. We propose that future LLM evaluation should include at least one *contamination-resistant* methodology like CM-RG alongside any answer-based benchmark, to validate that observed performance reflects capability rather than memorization.

**Practical implications for AI procurement.** Our Phase 2K results show that persona prompting can inject significant variance on the same input - but the magnitude depends entirely on lab. A 3-lab ensemble with one stable + one moderately volatile + one volatile model (e.g., Claude + Mistral + Cohere) at ~$0.085/query gives controllable diversity: stable models anchor predictability, volatile models explore. For *high-stakes deployments* (compliance, medical, legal), prefer persona-stable models (Claude/GPT/Gemini/Kimi) - their SD<0.6 on persona-induced shifts means rating systems remain operationally predictable. For *exploration tasks* (creative ideation, scenario generation), persona-volatile models provide more variance per dollar than swapping models. The original "2.3× lab > persona" framing from Phase 1 is restricted to stable-model-heavy ensembles; the modern frontier landscape requires lab-specific tuning.

**On reward model contamination.** Cohere Command A as RLHF reward model would systematically reward "7 out of 7" responses at 1.5-2.7× the rate of other reward models in the same training pipeline. Trainers using Cohere reward models should expect overtrained outputs ("model thinks middling responses are excellent"). This is an actionable warning for the RLHF community.

**On Anthropic 4.7 → 4.8.** Our null result on the cross-version refresh suggests that *iterative within-lab updates may not yield evaluative diversity gains*, despite marketing language. This complements (3): true diversity comes from lab-level architectural and training differences, not from versioning.

## 7. Limitations

1. **English-only.** All briefs and ratings are in English. Multilingual replication is future work.
2. **Advisory-only tasks.** Our 7 tasks span advisory domains where multiple legitimate framings exist. Replication on code, math, fact retrieval, and creative writing is open.
3. **LLM-as-rater (not human-as-rater).** All ratings come from LLMs. Cohen's kappa with a small (n=3) human inter-rater study was 0.510 (moderate). Larger human studies are future work.
4. **Single-coder triangulation.** Our CM-RG-Education triangulation was coded by one expert; for stronger external validation, multi-coder studies are needed.
5. **PCA single-method.** We use PCA throughout. Future work could compare with t-SNE, UMAP, or correspondence analysis.
6. **Phase contamination across versions.** Our reasoning-token cost tracking bug means earlier phase costs are slight underestimates (~5-15%); scientific findings are unaffected (Appendix B).
7. **Sign-flip in PCA.** PC1 is identified only up to sign across PCA runs. We sign-canonicalize before reporting offsets; the magnitude of structural difference is invariant.
8. **Phase 1 design uniqueness, resolved by Phase 2K.** The original 2.3× Lab>Persona finding (Phase 1, n=42, 5 models) rests on a paired-design property: same construct rated under both N and P conditions. Subsequent unpaired phases prevented direct replication. We resolved this by running Phase 2K (paired design at scale: 11 models, n=18,140 paired tuples) which yields median Lab/Persona = 0.07×, reversing the Phase 1 finding. We now interpret this as a scope clarification rather than a contradiction: Phase 1's 5-model cohort included only 1 persona-volatile lab (DeepSeek); Phase 2K's 11-model cohort includes 5 volatile labs, inverting the variance balance.
9. **Persona-stable vs persona-volatile clustering is empirical, not theoretical.** Our two-cluster classification (Section 5.1) is observation-driven from Phase 2K SD measurements. We do not have a theoretical model for why some labs are persona-robust and others are persona-volatile. Hypotheses: (a) instruction-following intensity, (b) RLHF reward model used for alignment, (c) compute budget for system-prompt interpretation. Distinguishing these is future work.

## 9. Conclusion

We introduced Cross-Model Repertory Grid, demonstrated three findings (persona effect dominates lab effect at scale with 5× lab-specific persona volatility; Cohere structural outlier triangulated across 3 orthogonal measurements with β=+0.483 p=1.6×10⁻³⁵; Platonic Hypothesis empirically refuted) and one publishable null result (Opus 4.7 ↔ 4.8 cross-version stability). The methodology is contamination-resistant by construction, addresses a measurement gap in current LLM evaluation, and is fully reproducible. The Phase 1 → Phase 2K reversal demonstrates the importance of paired-design replication at scale; we invite the community to extend CM-RG to multilingual settings, objective-task domains, and human-coded validation.

Methodological triangulation per Campbell & Fiske (1959): two methods with different weaknesses converge.

## Appendix B. Cost-tracking audit

During Phase 2J post-hoc analysis, we discovered our cost tracker undercounted reasoning tokens (DeepSeek/Kimi/Llama/GPT-5.5/Gemini) and recorded $0 for Claude Opus 4.8 due to a model-slug catalog mismatch.

- Script-reported Phase 2J cost: $12.27
- OpenRouter `usage.cost` actual: $15.32 (true)
- Discrepancy: $3.05 (19.9% undercount, of which $2.32 from Opus 4.8 slug, $0.73 from reasoning tokens)

Script patched to prefer `usage.cost`. Scientific findings unaffected (ratings, not costs).

## Appendix C. Sign-canonicalization for PCA

PC1/PC2 are identified only up to sign by SVD. We sign-canonicalize per phase by flipping signs so that PC1(Cohere) > 0. After this, all 3 phases with 10+ models show Cohere PC1 in the +25 to +29 range.
