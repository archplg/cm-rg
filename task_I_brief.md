# Task I: National scientific funding priority

## Context

You are advising the leadership of a national research funding agency (annual budget ~$8B, supports ~12,000 grants/year across all sciences). The agency is undergoing a 10-year strategic review and must choose **one primary funding priority** that will receive elevated weight in selection (estimated 30-40% of total budget over the decade) while keeping other portfolios continued at maintenance levels. This is a real "betting on the future" decision: where will the biggest scientific or societal returns come from?

Five candidate priorities have emerged from extensive consultation. **Choose one as the primary priority for the decade.** This will define which grant calls receive elevated funding, which infrastructure investments happen, and which discipline gets the most prestigious "Center of Excellence" awards.

## Options

**Option A: Climate-and-energy science (mitigation, adaptation, decarbonization)**
Concentrate elevated funding on climate models, materials for energy transition, carbon capture, agricultural adaptation, climate-health epidemiology. Outcomes: aligned with planetary-scale urgency; clear societal mission; large talent pool already mobilized. Risks: climate science is already well-funded relative to under-explored areas; output may be incremental rather than breakthrough; political vulnerability to climate-skeptic regimes.

**Option B: Biomedical engineering and aging biology**
Concentrate on understanding aging at molecular level, regenerative medicine, neurotech for cognitive decline, organ-on-chip platforms, advanced therapeutics. Outcomes: huge potential societal return (1-2 year increase in healthy lifespan = trillions in welfare); aligned with demographic transition; rapidly maturing field. Risks: expensive infrastructure; ethical complexity (longevity disparity); IP often captured by private sector; not addressing acute crises.

**Option C: Foundational physics and cosmology**
Concentrate on quantum gravity, dark matter direct detection, next-generation colliders, space telescopes, mathematical physics. Outcomes: civilizational-scale curiosity; trains highest-quality theoretical minds; possible foundational breakthroughs decades hence; aligned with "blue sky" research tradition. Risks: very long return horizon (often >50 years); difficult to articulate societal value to taxpayers; vulnerable to budget cuts in austerity periods; "particle physics" criticized as diminishing returns.

**Option D: AI safety and computational social science**
Concentrate on understanding AI systems (interpretability, alignment, evaluation), governance frameworks, social-science studies of AI impact on labor/democracy/inequality. Outcomes: addresses arguably the most consequential technology of the era; intersects with multiple disciplines; high-leverage if successful. Risks: field is young and partially captured by private labs (competing access); methods are unsettled; difficult to evaluate grant quality with limited senior reviewers; possibly redundant with private sector.

**Option E: Microbial and ecosystem science**
Concentrate on microbiome (human, soil, ocean), biodiversity genomics, ecosystem services valuation, soil health, planetary microbiology. Outcomes: under-funded relative to potential return (agricultural productivity, carbon sequestration, disease ecology); strong cross-cutting impact; aligned with planetary-health framing. Risks: lower visibility than climate or biomedicine; harder to translate to political wins; community is fragmented; some economic returns are diffuse (ecosystem services hard to monetize).

## What to provide

Choose one as the primary priority. Provide 200-400 words of reasoning. Advocate; do not enumerate. Address: why this priority, why not the most compelling alternative, and what specific scientific or societal outcome by 2036 would make this choice a clear success.
